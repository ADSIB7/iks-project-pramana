Measurement Systems of Ancient India

Blending Sacred Tradition with Exact Science

Nimesha

The briefest unit, equal to the blink of an eye. Forms the discrete basis for all ancient time tracking.

Kshana

Equivalent to 1.6 modern seconds. Historically used to measure momentary fractions of rituals and phenomena.

Ghati

Equivalent to exactly 24 modern minutes. Historically measured precisely by a sinking water bowl.

Muhurta

Consists of 2 Ghatis (48 minutes). Integral for planning, with exactly 30 Muhurtas forming a full solar day.

Angula

A finger's breadth. The fundamental standard unit across ancient architectural and monumental blueprints.

Hasta

A forearm's length, or 24 Angulas. A versatile measure used for daily commerce, textiles, and minor structures.

Dhanus

Equivalent to a bow-length (4 Hastas). Employed extensively in land surveying and town demarcations.

Yojana

A geographic measure (≈ 12-15 km). Used by ancient astronomers to describe terrestrial and cosmic distances.

Ratti

The remarkably highly uniform seed of the Gunja plant. The ultimate natural benchmark for early orangesmiths.

Masha

Equivalent to 8 Rattis (approx. 0.97g). Used predominantly in early Ayurvedic medicine prescriptions.

Tola

Valued at 12 Mashas (approx. 11.66g). A robust standard surviving even into modern South Asian metal markets.

Pala

A larger mass equalling 4 Tolas. Extensively utilized in weighing agricultural yields and bulk apothecary items.

Curious Revelations

Water Clocks

Hover to reveal

Ancient Indians tracked time with Ghatika Yantras—hemispherical copper bowls with a precise hole that sank entirely after exactly 24 minutes.

The Perfect Seed

Hover to reveal

Ratti seeds (Abrus precatorius) were used because their weight varies by less than 5% anywhere in nature, forming the perfect prehistoric standard.

Measuring Cosmos

Hover to reveal

Aryabhata used Yojanas to calculate the Earth's circumference in the 5th century, achieving an accuracy remarkably close to modern geodetic measurements.
